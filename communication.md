# Trends in U.S. Cities Hazard Identification and Climate Initiatives in CDP Reporting (2019-2023)
### Project Overview and Summary of Findings

## Why This Question Matters
Cities are where climate change hits hardest. More than half the world's population lives in urban areas, and that share is growing. When a heat wave strikes, when floodwaters rise, or when a major storm makes landfall, it is city governments that are on the front lines coordinating emergency response, protecting vulnerable residents, and planning for a future that looks increasingly different from the past.

But how well are cities actually doing this? Are they identifying the right risks? Are they building the programs needed to respond? Did the COVID-19 pandemic, which consumed enormous municipal resources and attention, set that work back? This project examined five years of climate reporting data from 18 of the largest U.S. cities in an attempt to find out.

## What Data Was Used

Cities around the world voluntarily report their climate risks and actions to the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP), one of the most widely used frameworks for tracking municipal climate progress. Each year, participating cities answer detailed questions about what hazards they face, whether they have formally assessed their climate risks, and how many adaptation programs they are running, among many other questions realted to climate issues. 

This analysis focuses on CDP data from 2019 to 2023, covering 18 of the most populous U.S. cities — including New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Houston, and Phoenix, among others. In total, the dataset captures 90 city-year snapshots, meaning each city's responses were examined across each of the five reporting years.

## What Was Found

### 1. Cities are taking climate risk assessment more seriously

In 2019, roughly one in four of the cities in this dataset had not yet completed a formal climate risk assessment — a structured process through which a city identifies what climate hazards it faces and how vulnerable its population and infrastructure are to those hazards. By 2022, every city in the dataset had completed one, and that held through 2023. This is a meaningful shift. Climate risk assessments are the foundation of effective adaptation planning, as you cannot prepare for what you have not acknowledged. The fact that this practice has become effectively universal among major U.S. cities over just five years suggests that climate preparedness is increasingly treated as a core institutional responsibility, rather than an optional initiative.

### 2. Adaptation programs have grown steadily over time

Alongside the rise in risk assessment completion, the number of climate adaptation programs cities reported running also increased over the study period. On average, cities reported around 6 adaptation programs per year in 2019. By 2022, that figure had risen to over 7, and it remained at that level through 2023. This upward trend was broad-based rather than being driven by one or two outlier cities pulling the average up, it was reflected growth across the dataset as a whole. This suggests that cities are not just identifying climate risks on paper, but they are increasingly building programs to address them.

### 3. The COVID-19 pandemic left a visible mark, but climate work recovered

The pandemic created an unprecedented strain on city governments beginning in 2020. Budgets were redirected, staff were redeployed, and political attention shifted almost entirely to public health emergency response. The data reflects this disruption: adaptation program counts dipped slightly in 2020 before recovering and ultimately surpassing pre-pandemic levels by 2022.

More striking is what happened to hazard identification. The number of climate hazards cities formally reported peaked in 2021, just as pandemic pressures were beginning to ease, and then dropped sharply in 2022 and remained flat through 2023. Statistical analysis confirmed that this decline was not a random fluctuation, it was a significant and consistent downward trend.

What explains it? The data alone cannot say for certain. It may reflect cities becoming more selective and refined in how they categorize and report hazards over time. It may reflect pandemic-era disruptions to the staff and processes involved in climate reporting, or it may signal that adaptation efforts have become sufficiently institutionalized that cities no longer feel the need to expand their hazard lists year over year in order to justify their programs. Further research incorporating budget and staffing data would be needed to untangle these possibilities.

### 4. Extreme heat is the defining climate hazard for major U.S. cities

Across all five years of data, extreme heat was the most frequently reported primary climate hazard,   appearing more often than any other hazard type by a significant margin, followed by extreme precipitation and flooding. This finding is consistent with broader national trends and carries serious implications. Extreme heat is one of the deadliest climate hazards, disproportionately affecting elderly residents, outdoor workers, low-income households without access to air conditioning, and communities of color. Its dominance in these city reports underscores the urgency of heat-specific adaptation strategies — from expanding urban tree canopy and cooling centers to redesigning building codes and public health early warning systems.

### 5. Bigger cities might not necessarily better prepared

It might seem intuitive that larger, denser cities, with more resources, larger budgets, and greater institutional capacity, would run more climate adaptation programs or identify more hazards than their smaller counterparts. However, this data does not support this assumption. Statistical tests found no meaningful relationship between a city's population size or population density and either the number of hazards it identified or the number of adaptation programs it reported. New York City, the largest city in the dataset by far, reported a number of adaptation programs close to the dataset average. Meanwhile, some mid-sized cities reported comparably robust climate programming. This finding suggests that what drives climate adaptation effort is not simply how big a city is. Factors like political leadership, access to federal funding, the strength of local climate advocacy, and institutional culture may matter far more than population size alone.

## What This Means

Taken together, these findings paint a cautiously optimistic picture of urban climate preparedness among major U.S. cities. Formal risk assessment has become standard practice, adaptation programming has grown, and cities appear to have survived the COVID-19 disruption without permanently abandoning their climate commitments.

At the same time, the post-2021 decline in hazard identification is also notable. If cities are reporting fewer hazards not because their risk profiles have genuinely changed, but because of reporting fatigue, shifting political priorities, or resource constraints, that is a signal worth taking seriously.

Additionally, the dominance of extreme heat as the defining urban climate hazard is a reminder that the work is far from done. Knowing a hazard exists is not the same as being prepared for it.

## Important Caveats

This analysis has limitations that are worth noting. It covers only 18 large U.S. cities, so findings may not apply to smaller or mid-sized municipalities, which often face tighter budgets and fewer staff resources, or cities outside the U.S. which presumably face a different political and cultural landscape. The CDP survey is voluntary, meaning cities that participate may already be more climate-engaged than those that do not, which could skew the overall picture to look somewhat more positive than reality. Population data was standardized to 2023 figures and applied across all years, which limits the ability to account for how city growth may have shaped reporting over time. Lastly, the most recent CDP data available is from 2023, leaving a gap between this analysis and the present day.
